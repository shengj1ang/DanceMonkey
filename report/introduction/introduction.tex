Brain--machine interfaces (BMIs) aim to translate neural activity into intended motor outputs~\cite{hochberg2012reach}. This work addresses motor cortical hand-trajectory decoding in a macaque reaching task, aiming to estimate two-dimensional hand trajectories to one of eight targets from spike trains recorded from 98 motor cortical units. The decoder follows a strictly causal approach: predictions begin at 320~ms and are updated every 20~ms using only past and current neural observations.

A natural baseline is direction-conditioned decoding. Motor cortical neurons encode movement direction at both the single-neuron and population levels~\cite{georgopoulos1982relations,georgopoulos1986neuronal}, and preparatory activity can carry information about the intended direction before movement onset~\cite{alexander1990preparation}. Motivated by these findings, we developed a first decoder that estimates movement direction from early spiking activity and then predicts the trajectory using direction-specific velocity regression followed by Kalman-filter-based state estimation, consistent with classical state-space decoding approaches~\cite{wu2003neural,gilja2012high}.

However, motor cortical direction encoding is not stationary across a trial. Single-neuron tuning can evolve dynamically over time~\cite{rickert2009dynamic}, and preparatory and movement-related population activity can occupy nearly orthogonal subspaces~\cite{elsayed2016reorganization}, suggesting that a single global decoder may have to compromise across different neural regimes. Moreover, motor cortical population activity is often organised in low-dimensional neural manifolds~\cite{gallego2017neural}, and low-dimensional latent dynamics can support accurate kinematic decoding~\cite{aghagolzadeh2016inference}. These observations motivated our second decoder, the Time-Dependent PCA Decoder, which constructs a separate low-dimensional representation at each causal time point. It then performs direction-conditioned trajectory prediction and softly combines predictions across all eight candidate directions based on their directional posterior probabilities, similar in spirit to mixture-of-experts gating~\cite{jacobs1991adaptive}. By comparing these two decoders, we test whether exploiting time-varying, low-dimensional population structure improves causal trajectory decoding.
